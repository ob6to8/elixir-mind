# The Toulmin model of argument

Stephen Toulmin's *The Uses of Argument* (1958) rejected the idea that formal,
Plato-style logic captures how real arguments work, proposing instead a
six-part anatomy of **practical argument** — emphasizing *justification* over
*inference*, and the contextual "testing and sifting of already existing
ideas" over universal principles.

## The six components

| Component | Role |
|---|---|
| **Claim** | the conclusion whose merit must be established |
| **Grounds / data** | the facts appealed to as foundation for the claim |
| **Warrant** | the statement *authorizing the movement* from grounds to claim |
| **Backing** | credentials certifying the warrant, when the warrant itself is challenged |
| **Qualifier** | the asserted degree of force ("probably", "certainly") |
| **Rebuttal** | acknowledged restrictions and exceptions to the claim |

Claim, grounds, and warrant form the essential skeleton; backing, qualifier,
and rebuttal strengthen it as needed. Originally aimed at legal reasoning, the
model entered rhetoric and academic debate via Brockriede and Ehninger (1963).

## Why it is in this brain

Toulmin supplies the key structural insight for any belief-graph design: the
**justification is a first-class object**, not just an edge. A warrant is
exactly the license attached to an inference step — premises → conclusion,
*because* of a stated rule — which is why belief/justification graphs are
naturally [bipartite](/glossary/bipartite-graph.md): one conclusion can be
held up by several independent warranted justifications, and consensus
evaluation needs to see them separately. The qualifier and rebuttal slots also
prefigure confidence annotation and defeasibility. GSN
[assurance cases](/knowledge-management/argumentation/assurance-cases-and-gsn.md)
are a direct engineering descendant. See the
[belief-decomposition analysis](/meta/analysis/belief-decomposition-derived-vs-authored.md).
